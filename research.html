The Long-term Impact of Public Health Measures Targeting Children

Abstract: This paper estimates the long-run impact of childhood exposure to public health initiatives on adult human capital. From 1908 to 1933 public and private agencies in the United States instituted county-level health departments (CHDs) that provided sanitation and health services geared toward children. To estimate the long-term benefits, I exploit variation in the timing, location, and age of CHD exposure. Children treated under the age of five show later-life earnings improvements of three to four percent. I investigate the mechanisms underlying this effect and demonstrate that higher earnings emerge from better adult health, measured by cognition and body mass index.

Explaining Declines in US Rural Mortality, 1910-1933: The Role of County Health Departments

Revision requested, Explorations in Economic History

Current Version: [ pdf | poster ]

Abstract: This study assesses the impact of an American rural public health intervention on child mortality over the years 1908 to 1933. Due to the absence of sanitation and child-oriented health services outside of urban areas, public and private agencies sponsored county-level health departments (CHDs) throughout the United States. Population health improvements are identified through variation in the location and timing of investments. Results reveal mortality declines for infants and young children under the age of five. For the general population, CHDs prevent roughly three child deaths per 1,000 births. Nonwhite infants are disproportionately benefited and experience a reduction by seven deaths per 1,000 births.

The Political Economy of Local Public Health: Progressive Era Influence in Rural Areas

Abstract: This paper examines the factors that shaped the appropriation of rural public health through county health departments (CHDs) in the early twentieth century. Using two measures of Progressive influence, third party votes and attendance at a social gospel church, the analysis tests whether CHD adoption is related to the Progressive Era agendas. Results find that participating states are more Progressive, while individual counties are less aligned with Progressive thought. Instead, local county governments appear to allocate funds according to their ability instead of their need. Area-specific health conditions do not predict county CHD activity. Instead, local support is predicted by the area's propensity to provide other public goods, as well as the taxable values of income and land. This distribution of CHDs in wealthy areas is partially counteracted through grants from outside public and private agencies. The donation patterns of external sources appear unrelated to area-specific social, political, and demographic factors."
